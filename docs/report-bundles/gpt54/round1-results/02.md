I checked your notes plus primary sources across arXiv, OpenReview/NeurIPS/ICLR, CVPR/ICCV/WACV, PMLR/ICML, NDSS, and USENIX. My main read is that “beyond PIA/TMIA-DM” is now a real space, but the strongest 2024–2025 directions are **not** more trajectory-consistency variants. The best-supported lines are: **single-step score signals**, **frequency-aware residual signals**, and **low-FPR calibration/fusion**. On defense, the field is drifting toward **selective or structured mitigation** rather than uniform randomization. ([arXiv][1])

That matches your benchmark pressure. Your current admitted DDPM/CIFAR10 surface is still PIA-centric, and your own notes show low-FPR metrics matter more than headline AUC; the current stochastic-dropout defense already hurts TPR@1%FPR and TPR@0.1%FPR more than AUC, so the next wins are most likely to come from **tail-aware scoring** and **targeted defenses**, not just another average-case score.    

A second important correction: true gradients are still white-box under your own threat model. So if you pursue a “gradient-like” idea in gray-box, it should be **finite-difference sensitivity of score outputs**, not autograd over parameters or inputs. And CLiD is real and strong, but it is tailored to **text-image conditional overfitting**, so it is not the first thing I would port to your unconditional DDPM/CIFAR10 surface.   ([NeurIPS 会议记录][2])

## Survey map

1. **Direct non-PIA attack papers now exist.**
   CLiD (NeurIPS 2024) uses conditional likelihood discrepancy for text-to-image models; 2025 work pushes frequency-domain MIAs; and SimA (2025) turns gray-box membership into a single-query score-norm attack. Black-box work also moved forward through variation-API and reconstruction-style attacks. ([NeurIPS 会议记录][2])

2. **The richest signal source is the memorization-measurement literature.**
   NeMo, cross-attention memorization, Bright Ending, InvMM, and the sharpness/geometric papers are not just “interpretability”; they say where memorization lives: in a few neurons, local patches, inversion stability, and sharp local basins. That is exactly the material you can turn into new gray-box audit signals. ([OpenReview][3])

3. **Defense papers are moving away from blanket perturbation.**
   AMG, prediction-magnitude detection/mitigation, attraction-basin guidance, PRSS, and MemControl all try to localize mitigation in time, condition, or model subspace. On the security side, the diffusion-specific hits I found were mostly NDSS black-box attacks and USENIX defense/training papers such as dp-promise, rather than a direct gray-box PIA successor from S&P/USENIX. ([arXiv][4])

I would also treat **ranking-sensitive variables** as an **objective**, not a separate attack family. In your setup they belong inside calibration/fusion, because low-FPR is the regime you care about and where the current defense already bites hardest.   

## Hypothesis 1: SimA-style single-query score norm

* **Signal/Method**: At one chosen timestep (t^*), use the norm of the predicted noise or score, (|\epsilon_\theta(x_{t^*}, t^*)|), optionally standardized by timestep noise level, as the membership score. The 2025 SimA paper gives the direct theory: the denoiser points toward a kernel-weighted local mean of nearby training samples, so its norm carries proximity-to-training-set information. ([arXiv][1])
* **Intuition**: This is the cleanest non-trajectory gray-box challenger. It uses one query, no pairwise trajectory consistency, and no attack-duration switching. It also lines up with your own activation-based track, where single-timestep score norms already appear as a plausible gray-box signal. ([arXiv][1])  
* **Literature support**: SimA is the direct anchor; the sharpness and geometry papers reinforce the same broader point from a different angle, namely that memorization is local geometry, not only full-path consistency. ([arXiv][1])
* **Execution plan**: Sweep 4–6 candidate timesteps on a small 128/128 calibration split, lock the best low-FPR setting, then do the 512/512 run. If plain norm is weak, add two cheap companions: cosine between (\epsilon_\theta) and (x_t), and norm variance under tiny input jitter.  
* **Cost**: Roughly 1–6 model queries per sample, versus your current PIA budget of 60 forward passes per sample. This is the cheapest serious challenger for an RTX 4070 8GB setup.  
* **Distinctness**: Not trajectory consistency, not TMIA-style temporal thresholding, not white-box gradients. This is the first thing I would implement.   

## Hypothesis 2: Mid-frequency calibrated residual attack

* **Signal/Method**: Build a short reconstruction or inversion residual, then score only the **mid-frequency** band of that residual, using band-pass L2 or band-pass SSIM. The 2025 frequency papers argue that existing MIAs miss diffusion’s frequency asymmetry, and FCRE shows that band-selective residuals reduce difficulty confounding. ([arXiv][5])
* **Intuition**: Low frequencies are too easy and high frequencies are too noisy. Mid-band structure is where member/non-member differences can survive while nuisance variance drops. That is exactly the kind of signal that can help your low-FPR tail. ([arXiv][6]) 
* **Literature support**: The direct anchors are the 2025 frequency-domain MIA paper and the 2025 FCRE paper; CoprGuard independently shows that diffusion outputs preserve training-data spectral statistics, which is consistent with spectral provenance signals being useful. ([arXiv][5])
* **Execution plan**: Reuse your current reconstruction surface, transform residuals with DCT/FFT, retain an annular mid-frequency band, and evaluate a 1-step and short-5-step variant. Use 128/128 to choose the band, then freeze it for 512/512.  
* **Cost**: GPU-light plus cheap CPU FFTs; if you keep the loop short, it should stay below PIA-scale cost. 
* **Distinctness**: The signal is spectral residual structure, not epsilon consistency over a denoising path. ([arXiv][5])

## Hypothesis 3: Tail-calibrated tri-signal fusion

* **Signal/Method**: Fuse PIA + SimA + frequency score, or PIA + SecMI + SimA, by converting each score into a nonmember-calibrated p-value or difficulty-adjusted quantile, then combine them with a monotone low-FPR objective. ([Proceedings of Machine Learning Research][7])
* **Intuition**: Your benchmark is tail-sensitive, and your current defense already moves the tail much more than AUC. Calibration papers keep finding that raw scores fail mostly on hard non-members, which is exactly what kills TPR@1%FPR and TPR@0.1%FPR.    ([arXiv][8])
* **Literature support**: ICML 2024 quantile regression for diffusion MIAs, LDC-MIA for low-FPR improvement, RAPID’s critique of naïve difficulty calibration, ACMIA, and the 2025 reliability/stability ensemble paper all point in the same direction: better calibration often beats inventing yet another uncalibrated raw score. ([Proceedings of Machine Learning Research][7])
* **Execution plan**: This is the cleanest path around your G1-A blocker. You do not need TMIA-DM to land an honest fused scorer; you only need signals that all run on the same frozen 512-sample surface. I would start with PIA + SimA, then add the frequency score only if it lifts partial AUC.   
* **Cost**: Nearly CPU-only once score dumps exist. Highest expected ROI per engineering hour. 
* **Distinctness**: Lower scientific novelty than a brand-new signal, but probably the fastest path to a measurable low-FPR gain. ([Proceedings of Machine Learning Research][7])

## Hypothesis 4: Suspicion-gated perturbation defense

* **Signal/Method**: Use a cheap detector such as SimA norm or a frequency residual at an early timestep. Only when suspicion is high do you perturb the denoiser later, via small epsilon noise, step skipping, or structured late-step dropout. ([arXiv][1])
* **Intuition**: The 2024–2025 mitigation papers keep converging on the same idea: memorization is localized, so selective intervention should preserve utility better than uniform intervention. That makes this the natural successor to your current all-steps stochastic-dropout baseline. ([arXiv][4])  
* **Literature support**: AMG applies mitigation selectively and even discusses DDPM/CIFAR10-compatible settings; the 2024 detection/mitigation paper detects memorization early from prediction magnitude; attraction-basin and PRSS papers argue for targeted guidance changes instead of blanket perturbation. ([arXiv][4])
* **Execution plan**: Score every sample at a cheap probe timestep, route only the top-q suspicious cases into a perturbed late-step schedule, and compare against current all-steps dropout at a matched quality budget. On unconditional DDPM, translate the guidance papers into denoiser perturbation or step scheduling rather than CFG-specific logic. ([arXiv][9]) 
* **Cost**: Small online overhead, plausibly in the same ballpark as your current ~5% defense overhead if the detector stays cheap. 
* **Distinctness**: Sample-aware and event-driven, not blanket stochasticity. ([arXiv][4])

## Hypothesis 5: Structured leakage-channel suppression

* **Signal/Method**: On a small calibration set, rank blocks or channels by how separable their activation summaries are for members vs non-members, then damp only the worst offenders at inference. ([OpenReview][3])
* **Intuition**: NeMo, cross-attention memorization, and Bright Ending all say leakage can be highly localized. If that transfers even partially to CIFAR U-Nets, structured suppression should beat random dropout at equal perturbation budget. ([OpenReview][3])
* **Literature support**: NeMo localizes memorization down to neurons; MemAttn links memorization to concentrated token attention; Bright Ending localizes memorized patches; MemControl argues that overcapacity is part of the problem. ([OpenReview][3])
* **Execution plan**: Dump activation summaries on 128/128, compute per-channel leakage AUROC, mask the top 0.5–5%, then rerun PIA, SimA, and the fused scorer. Keep it owner-side only unless you explicitly broaden the threat model.  
* **Cost**: One extra logging pass for calibration; near-zero runtime overhead once the mask is fixed. 
* **Distinctness**: Very different from PIA/TMIA-DM, but also the most speculative and the least “pure gray-box” under your stricter definition. I would label it **gray-box-plus / owner-instrumented**.  

## What I would run first

1. **SimA-style single-query score norm.** It is the cleanest direct challenger, cheapest to test, and most honest under your current gray-box definition. ([arXiv][1]) 
2. **PIA + SimA tail-calibrated fusion.** This is the fastest plausible low-FPR lift and does not wait on the TMIA-DM 512-sample gap. ([Proceedings of Machine Learning Research][7]) 
3. **Mid-frequency residual score.** This is the most promising genuinely new signal after SimA. ([arXiv][5])
4. **Suspicion-gated perturbation defense.** Best next defense experiment after all-steps dropout. ([arXiv][4])
5. **Structured channel suppression**, but only if activation logging is already easy. ([OpenReview][3])

Three things I would de-prioritize for now: true gradients unless you intentionally switch to white-box; CLiD on unconditional DDPM/CIFAR10; and full training-time defenses as your first 8GB experiment. The strongest training-time anchors I found are dp-promise (USENIX Security 2024), SMP-LoRA for LDM adaptation (AAAI 2025), MemControl (WACV 2025), and MIST as a general MIA defense, but they all move you away from a bounded audit-time experiment toward retraining or adaptation cycles.   ([USENIX][10])

If I had one 4-hour slot on your current surface, I would spend it on **SimA scan → PIA+SimA low-FPR fusion → suspicion-gated defense against that fused scorer**. That gives you one new signal, one new scorer, and one new defense without waiting for TMIA-DM assets or relaxing the threat model.
