Your repo state already makes the gap very clear: GSA on DDPM/CIFAR10 is essentially saturated at AUC 0.998192, the bounded loss-score line is much weaker at AUC 0.699463 with TPR@1%FPR 0.03125, DPDM drops GSA to near-random at AUC 0.488783, and the current DDPM Finding NeMo-style intervention was a negative falsifier rather than a positive defense. That means the best next step is not “another loss variant,” but a genuinely different signal family or a post-training defense.    

After a fresh sweep across arXiv, OpenReview/ICLR/ICML/NeurIPS, and CVPR/ICCV sources, my ranking is: activation/subspace attacks first, score-vector geometry second as the fastest practical win, curvature/influence third as the strongest “paper” bet, then post-training subspace editing and targeted unlearning on defense. The main reason is that recent work gives direct support for neuron/subspace localization, score-output geometry, and diffusion-specific influence approximations, while inference-time prompt/sampler tricks do not really close your white-box threat model. ([arXiv][1])

## Hypothesis 1: Activation-subspace fingerprint attack

* **Signal/Method:** Extract forward activations at a few fixed DDPM layers and timesteps, then build a compact fingerprint from per-channel statistics such as mean absolute activation, variance, sparsity, and a PCA/SVD sketch. Use a simple shadow-calibrated logistic regressor or small MLP on the selected channels. This is the cleanest “not gradient, not loss” white-box family. ([arXiv][1])

* **Intuition:** Recent white-box MIA work shows informative neurons alone can materially improve attack strength, and diffusion memorization work shows memorization can localize to individual neurons or a narrow shared subspace rather than being spread uniformly through the model. ([arXiv][1])

* **Literature support:**

  1. *Unveiling the Unseen* (2024) identifies informative neurons and reports up to a 26% improvement over prior white-box MIA baselines. ([arXiv][1])
  2. *Finding NeMo* (NeurIPS 2024) argues that, in diffusion models, single neurons can be responsible for memorization of individual samples. ([arXiv][2])
  3. *Memorized Images in Diffusion Models share a Subspace that can be Located and Deleted* (2024) finds that memorized prompts activate a common narrow subspace and proposes post-hoc pruning around it. ([arXiv][3])

* **Execution plan:** Reuse your current localization bridge, but do not repeat the single-selector setup. Start from the same neighborhood you already probe, such as `mid_block.attentions.0.to_v`, then add 2 adjacent blocks and 3 timesteps. Stream activations for 64 members and 64 controls per target/shadow, rank channels by member-vs-control effect size, then train LR/MLP on the top 32 to 128 features. Use `max_samples=64` only as a scout, then promote only if the signal survives at 256 or full scale, because your own notes show low-FPR is much less stable than AUC in bounded packets.    

* **Cost:** Rough pilot on a 4070 8 GB: about 0.5 to 1.5 GPU hours for extraction across target + 3 shadows, then minutes on CPU for feature selection and training.

* **Distinctness:** Pure forward-pass internal-state family. It is the best fit for your “second family beyond GSA” requirement.

## Hypothesis 2: Score-vector geometry attack

* **Signal/Method:** Use the predicted noise or score vector itself, not the loss. Aggregate a few norms/angles across selected timesteps, optionally after suppressing high-frequency image components or using low-pass reconstructed errors as auxiliary features. ([arXiv][4])

* **Intuition:** The 2025 SimA paper shows the denoiser output points toward a kernel-weighted local mean of nearby training samples, so its norm carries membership information. A 2025 frequency analysis adds that existing diffusion MIAs are hurt by “high-frequency deficiency,” and simple filtering can improve AUC, ASR, and TPR@1%FPR with negligible overhead. Earlier ICLR 2024 work also tied memorization detection to score magnitude. ([arXiv][4])

* **Literature support:**

  1. *Score-based Membership Inference on Diffusion Models* (2025) proposes SimA, a single-query score-based attack that performs strongly on DDPMs. ([arXiv][4])
  2. *Unveiling Impact of Frequency Components on Membership Inference Attacks for Diffusion Models* (2025) shows high-frequency content adds variance and proposes a plug-and-play filter. ([arXiv][5])
  3. *Detecting, Explaining, and Mitigating Memorization in Diffusion Models* (ICLR 2024) links memorization detection to the magnitude of conditional predictions early in generation. ([OpenReview][6])

* **Execution plan:** This is the fastest bounded test. For each candidate image, run 4 to 8 timesteps, log `||eps_hat||_2`, maybe one cosine feature, and one low-pass reconstruction error. Calibrate a threshold or tiny LR on shadows. Because SimA is notably stronger on DDPMs than on LDMs, your DDPM/CIFAR10 asset is a favorable test bed. ([arXiv][4])

* **Cost:** Rough pilot: 0.25 to 0.75 GPU hours. Memory usually below what GSA needs.

* **Distinctness:** This is an output-vector family, not gradients or scalar loss. One caveat: in the literature it sits closer to the “grey-box/common diffusion MIA” line than a pure internal-neuron white-box novelty, so I would treat it as a very strong auxiliary family rather than the cleanest flagship second family. ([arXiv][4])

## Hypothesis 3: Curvature-preconditioned self-influence attack

* **Signal/Method:** Replace raw gradient aggregation with a second-order score such as self-influence (g^\top H^{-1} g), or a bounded approximation using diagonal Fisher, block-diagonal GGN, or K-FAC. This is the Hessian/influence family you flagged. ([arXiv][7])

* **Intuition:** The IHA work argues that, under SGD, optimal membership inference is genuinely white-box and needs parameter-side information beyond black-box loss. Diffusion-specific influence work shows K-FAC/GGN approximations can scale on diffusion models and outperform prior attribution baselines. ([arXiv][7])

* **Literature support:**

  1. *Do Parameters Reveal More than Loss for Membership Inference?* (2024) introduces IHA and argues optimal MI under SGD requires white-box access. ([arXiv][7])
  2. *Influence Functions for Scalable Data Attribution in Diffusion Models* (2024) develops K-FAC/GGN approximations tailored to diffusion. ([arXiv][8])
  3. *Intriguing Properties of Data Attribution on Diffusion Models* (ICLR 2024) already studies DDPMs on CIFAR-10, which makes this family much less speculative for your exact baseline. ([arXiv][9])

* **Execution plan:** Do a diagonal-Fisher pilot first, not full iHVP. If you already have gradient dumps from GSA, precondition them offline and compare `g^T F_diag^{-1} g` against raw gradient norms at one or two layers/timesteps. Only if the pilot beats raw gradient norm or loss should you upgrade to K-FAC/HVP. This is the right “novel paper bet,” but not the first engineering bet.

* **Cost:** If you reuse existing gradients, the pilot is mostly offline and cheap. If you do online K-FAC/HVP, budget roughly 1.5 to 4 GPU hours for a bounded 64/64 pilot.

* **Distinctness:** Borderline with GSA because gradients still appear inside the formula, but this is not a first-order gradient family. The signal is curvature-corrected parameter influence.

## Hypothesis 4: Training-free risky-subspace pruning defense

* **Signal/Method:** Use member-vs-control activation contrasts to identify a shared risky subspace, then remove it with projection or very sparse pruning. Do not repeat the current “single selector, top-k, alpha=0.5” intervention; switch to a multi-sample shared-subspace edit. 

* **Intuition:** The best recent diffusion defense story is that memorization is concentrated in a small neuron/subspace region, and post-hoc edits to that region can mitigate leakage without retraining the whole model. Your current negative DDPM Finding NeMo packet does not falsify this broader family; it falsifies one fixed-mask version of it.  ([arXiv][2])

* **Literature support:**

  1. *Finding NeMo* (NeurIPS 2024) localizes memorization neurons in diffusion models and shows deactivating them can prevent replication. ([arXiv][2])
  2. *Memorized Images in Diffusion Models share a Subspace that can be Located and Deleted* (2024) explicitly argues memorization lies in a shared subspace and proposes one-time post-hoc surgery. ([arXiv][3])
  3. *ConceptPrune* (2024) and *CURE* (2025) show training-free weight-space editing/pruning can be both efficient and robust in diffusion models. ([arXiv][10])

* **Execution plan:** Run H1 first, compute the top risky principal directions from members minus controls, and project them out in 1 to 2 layers. Try 3 edit types in order: channel zeroing, low-rank orthogonal projection, then sparse weight pruning of 0.05% to 0.5%. Re-evaluate both GSA and H1/H2. This is the best immediate defense candidate because it is post-training and actually addresses a white-box attacker.

* **Cost:** About 0.5 to 1 hour for identification, minutes for editing, then evaluation dominates.

* **Distinctness:** Strongest post-training defense family beyond DP-SGD.

## Hypothesis 5: Targeted unlearning / PEFT regularization defense

* **Signal/Method:** Build a small forget set from the highest-risk members under GSA/H1/H2, then do bounded fine-tuning with an unlearning objective or PEFT mask instead of retraining the full DDPM. ([arXiv][11])

* **Intuition:** If white-box leakage is concentrated in a minority of risky samples or directions, directly unlearning those samples or constraining capacity in the relevant layers is more promising than a global sampler tweak. Theory also now says the empirical score-matching minimizer itself can induce memorization unless regularized. ([arXiv][11])

* **Literature support:**

  1. *Data Unlearning in Diffusion Models* (ICLR 2025) introduces SISS and claims the first diffusion data-unlearning method with theoretical guarantees. ([arXiv][11])
  2. *MemControl* (WACV 2025) shows parameter-efficient fine-tuning with capacity control can trade off memorization and generation quality. ([CVF开放获取][12])
  3. *Memorization and Regularization in Generative Diffusion Models* (2025) gives the cleanest theory case for regularization, under-parameterization, and early stopping. ([arXiv][13])
  4. *Data Pruning in Generative Diffusion Models* (2024) suggests even simple clustering-based pruning can beat more complicated pruning rules. ([arXiv][14])

* **Execution plan:** Use the top 32 to 128 highest-risk members as a pseudo-forget set. Fine-tune only late/mid U-Net blocks or a PEFT mask for a short schedule with early stopping. Re-run GSA and H1/H2. This is the heavier defense, but it is the one most likely to produce a meaningful non-DP defense result if H4 is too weak.

* **Cost:** Roughly 2 to 4 GPU hours for a pilot on 4070 8 GB if you keep the forget set small and update only a small parameter subset.

* **Distinctness:** Strong defense family, but less lightweight than H4.

## What I would deprioritize

I would not spend another cycle on loss-threshold or loss-LR variants; your own repo already shows the loss-score line is weak at low FPR and Shadow-LR on loss is still hold-review-only and CPU-bound.  

I would also not count inference-time-only memorization guidance as a real solution for this repo’s white-box setting. Your threat model gives the attacker full access to weights, activations, gradients, and arbitrary forward/backward computation, so sampler-only defenses can simply be ignored. This is why I ranked H4/H5 above sampler-side methods like AMG or SAIL, even though those papers are useful for understanding memorization.  ([CVF开放获取][15])

## Recommended order

1. **Run H1 first.** It is the best fit for “distinct second family,” aligns with your existing observability bridge, and is cheap enough for a bounded scout.
2. **Run H2 in parallel.** It is the fastest likely win and gives you an immediate non-gradient baseline to compare against GSA and loss.
3. **If H1 produces a clean risky subspace, jump straight to H4.** That is the best post-training defense bet.
4. **Use H3 only after H1/H2.** It is the most novel but also the most engineering-heavy.
5. **Reserve H5 for when you want an actual defense result, not just a new attack family.**

The strongest combined research story is **H1 + H4**: use activation fingerprints to expose a real second white-box family, then turn the same signal into a shared-subspace edit defense. That is more novel than another loss variant, more compatible with DDPM/CIFAR10 than prompt-side text defenses, and more realistic for your white-box threat model than inference-only mitigation. Use 64-sample packets only for gating; promote only when the four-metric report survives at larger scale, because your own notes correctly warn that bounded AUC can look good while low-FPR collapses.
