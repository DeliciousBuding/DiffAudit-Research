Bottom line: the next I-C step should be **calibrated conditional evidence transfer**, not **agreement-first fusion**. Your own X-56 note already falsifies simple rank agreement as a cross-box support mechanism, and your evaluation notes already say fusion should be judged mainly by **low-FPR gain plus overhead**, not by AUC alone. Because GSA is already near ceiling in AUC but still imperfect at 1% FPR, the real headroom is in rescuing tail errors, not in making the full ROC prettier.    

On the 2024–2025 literature side, I did **not** find a mature paper that already solves end-to-end black/gray/white cross-box fusion for diffusion MIAs. The recent diffusion papers I found are still mostly **single-family attacks or single-family enhancements**: PIA for gray-box trajectories, REDIFFUSE for black-box variation APIs, CLiD for conditional overfitting in text-to-image diffusion, the MIDST 2025 winner’s lightweight MLP over multi-noise/time loss features, a 2025 frequency-domain plug-in for error-based MIAs, and SimA’s single-query score-based attack. That makes multimodal **late fusion, calibration, conflict discounting, and selective routing** the right outside literature to import into DiffAudit. ([OpenReview][1])

Three outside ideas matter most. First, **late fusion** is attractive when views are heterogeneous and resources are limited. Second, **dynamic/uncertainty-aware fusion** can improve reliability instead of blindly averaging. Third, for your low-FPR setting, recent calibration theory is more useful than plain ECE: ROC-regularized isotonic preserves ROC structure, Venn-Abers gives finite-sample calibrated set predictions, and Cutoff Calibration Error is explicitly closer to thresholded decision-making. I would use those ideas as scaffolding around your existing Bayes / information-theoretic / PAC notes. ([arXiv][2])   

A useful theory heuristic is: only fuse when the second signal seems to add **conditional information** beyond the first. Your own notes phrase that as (I(Y;B\mid A)), and a recent multimodal MI analysis reports that **lower inter-modality MI** can correlate with better performance, which is exactly the complementarity story you need. In practice, that means **GSA+PIA** and **Recon+PIA** are better bets than fusing closely related internal signals. ([arXiv][3])  

## Hypothesis 1: Calibrated Late Fusion of GSA + PIA

* **Fusion method**: Calibrate GSA and PIA separately on a held-out calibration split, then fuse the calibrated probabilities with a 2-feature logistic regression or weighted average. Start with isotonic; if you want stronger finite-sample behavior, try Venn-Abers on the shortlist. Use rank-transformed scores if raw scales drift across splits.   ([arXiv][2])
* **Intuition**: GSA is the strongest anchor, but its weakness is the low-FPR tail. PIA is much weaker overall, yet mechanistically different, so its value is not big AUC movement; it is the chance to rescue some GSA misses at 1% FPR.   
* **Literature support**: Recent late-fusion work explicitly argues that separate predictors can be combined lightweight at the output level; recent dynamic-fusion work gives a generalization argument plus relative calibration; recent calibration papers strengthen isotonic/Venn-style post-hoc calibration for downstream decisions. ([arXiv][2])
* **Execution plan**: Build a common score table on a 512-calibration / 512-test split. Fit per-signal calibrators on calibration, then compare: best single signal, simple average, weighted average, and 2-feature logistic regression. Report 5-fold cross-split mean/std before trying a 3-box version.  
* **Cost**: With your internal timing estimates, 1024 total samples is about **8.5 minutes for PIA** and **34 minutes for GSA**; calibration and fusion fitting are CPU-only and take minutes. This is well inside your 4h bound. 
* **Evaluation**: My target would be **ΔTPR@1%FPR ≈ +0.03 to +0.07** over GSA, with very small ΔAUC because GSA is already near ceiling. Your own DDPM/CIFAR10 fusion memo puts the full PIA+GSA hypothesis around **0.50–0.55 TPR@1%FPR** versus **0.453** for GSA alone, which is the right scale to test first. 

## Hypothesis 2: Support/Disconfirm with a Neutral Zone

* **Fusion method**: Convert each signal to calibrated probability or interval, then define three states: **support**, **disconfirm**, and **neutral**. Support/member only when both boxes clear confidence thresholds and conflict is low; disconfirm when one is confidently high and the other confidently low; neutral otherwise. In disconfirm/neutral, abstain or defer instead of forcing a binary call.  ([arXiv][4])
* **Intuition**: This is the cleanest successor to X-56. Your negative falsifier already says cross-box support should **not** mean same-object broad rank order; it should mean **conditional corroboration at high confidence**, with an explicit conflict bucket.  
* **Literature support**: Calibrated Selective Classification rejects “uncertain uncertainties,” likelihood-ratio selective classification gives a principled abstention rule, and discounted belief fusion explicitly discounts conflicting sources rather than averaging them. ([arXiv][5])
* **Execution plan**: Using cached scores only, grid-search thresholds for support/disconfirm/neutral on calibration data under **FPR ≤ 1%** and a minimum coverage target. Start with **GSA+PIA**, then repeat with **Recon+PIA**.  
* **Cost**: CPU-only once the score table exists. 
* **Evaluation**: Report **TPR@1%FPR**, abstention rate, selective risk/coverage, disagreement rate, and error-set Jaccard. Expect smaller raw AUC movement but better low-FPR honesty; net gain may be only **+0.02 to +0.05** if abstentions count as negatives, but materially larger on the accepted subset.  

## Hypothesis 3: Portable Recon + PIA Fusion, with Optional Frequency-Filtered Third Score

* **Fusion method**: Build a black/gray-only late-fusion track around **Recon + PIA**. If implementation is easy, add a third cheap score by frequency-filtering the error or trajectory signal before thresholding; the recent frequency-domain paper presents this as a plug-and-play enhancement for error-based diffusion MIAs.   ([arXiv][6])
* **Intuition**: This is the most portable cross-box path. Recon and PIA observe very different surfaces—final-output reconstruction versus denoising-trajectory consistency—so they are a better complementarity bet than two closely related internal features. It also keeps white-box access optional.  
* **Literature support**: REDIFFUSE expands the black-box surface using only a variation API, the 2025 frequency paper adds a plug-and-play filter, and SimA shows that additional cheap score families are still being discovered rather than exhausted. ([arXiv][7])
* **Execution plan**: On DDPM/CIFAR10, extract Recon and PIA on a common split, estimate disagreement/error overlap first, calibrate each signal, then compare weighted late fusion against best single. If the frequency-filtered plug-in can be implemented as pure postprocessing, test it as a third score.  
* **Cost**: Recon dominates. Over 1024 total samples, your internal timings imply about **170 minutes for Recon** plus **8.5 minutes for PIA**; a post-hoc frequency filter should add only CPU-minutes if it works on saved outputs. 
* **Evaluation**: Prioritize **ΔTPR@1%FPR**, query efficiency, and portability. A reasonable first target is **+0.04 to +0.07 TPR@1%FPR** over Recon alone; your internal baseline memo expects **Recon+PIA ≈ 0.15–0.18 TPR@1%FPR**, which is consistent with that target. 

## Hypothesis 4: Tail-Gated Low-FPR Cascade (GSA Anchor, PIA Only Near the Threshold)

* **Fusion method**: Use **GSA as the global scorer**. Only for samples near the GSA threshold that defines 1% FPR, or for the top GSA tail, consult **PIA** and require corroboration or at least non-contradiction. Outside that band, use GSA alone.  
* **Intuition**: GSA is too strong to replace, but too dominant to fuse blindly. The right question is whether PIA can **locally repair the decision boundary** where false positives and missed positives concentrate. This is a low-FPR intervention, not a whole-ROC intervention.  
* **Literature support**: Predictive Dynamic Fusion gives a theoretical argument for dynamic, confidence-aware fusion; selective-classification work supplies a principled abstention view; recent MoE/gating papers show that routing by sample difficulty or modality availability can improve robustness without always paying full fusion cost. ([Proceedings of Machine Learning Research][8])
* **Execution plan**: On calibration data, locate the GSA threshold at 1% FPR. Define a band around it or a top-quantile tail. Fit a small band-specific rule using GSA and PIA only there, and sweep the routed fraction (10%, 20%, 30%). This can be done entirely on cached scores before any new extraction policy is used online.  
* **Cost**: If PIA is consulted on only **20%** of samples, the added extraction overhead is about **5%** above always-GSA; even 30% routing stays bounded. 
* **Evaluation**: Report average cost, stage utilization, **ΔTPR@1%FPR**, and cost-benefit ratio. I would target **+0.02 to +0.05 TPR@1%FPR** over plain GSA for single-digit percentage overhead; if that fails, the same framework can still be kept as a cost-saver instead of a performance booster.  

## Hypothesis 5: Cross-Box Stacking, but Stop at Logistic Regression Unless the Data Clearly Supports More

* **Fusion method**: Train a meta-model on cached scores. Start with **elastic-net logistic regression** on calibrated scores, ranks, pairwise products, and disagreement spread. Only if cross-validation is stable, try a **tiny MLP** with dropout.  
* **Intuition**: Recent diffusion MIA work shows that lightweight learned combinations can beat hand heuristics, but your regime is small-(k) and probably moderate-(n). That makes logistic stacking the sweet spot; neural fusion is an optional last mile, not the starting point. ([arXiv][9])
* **Literature support**: The MIDST 2025 winner uses a lightweight MLP over multiple diffusion-derived features, Meta Fusion and MetaMMF motivate learned sample-dependent fusion, and your own PAC note warns that neural fusion needs much more calibration data than linear fusion. ([arXiv][9]) 
* **Execution plan**: Use 5-fold cross-validation on a shared score table. Compare weighted average, logistic stacking, and a tiny 2-layer MLP. Promote the MLP only if it improves **ΔTPR@1%FPR by >0.02** and that gain is stable across folds.  
* **Cost**: Pure CPU once the score table exists. Logistic is minutes. Tiny MLP is also cheap computationally; the real cost is overfitting risk, not runtime. 
* **Evaluation**: Report fold-to-fold variance, calibration quality, and weight stability in addition to performance. My expectation is that **logistic stacking captures most of the gain**; the MLP may add only **+0.00 to +0.03** beyond logistic if (n) is large enough, and negative transfer is plausible here.  

What I would run first: **Hypothesis 1** and **Hypothesis 2**. Hypothesis 1 is the highest-probability low-FPR win. Hypothesis 2 is the cleanest conceptual continuation of I-C after X-56. After that, I would choose between **Hypothesis 3** for portability and **Hypothesis 4** for cost-sensitive deployment. I would postpone the MLP part of Hypothesis 5 until the linear baselines and neutral-zone analysis are stable.
