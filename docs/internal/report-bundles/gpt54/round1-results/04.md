Your current repo already shows the core trade-off: cheap stochastic-dropout only modestly weakens gray-box PIA, while DPDM/DP-SGD nearly neutralizes white-box GSA but at full-retraining cost. The 2024–2025 defense literature for diffusion-model MIA is still much thinner than the attack literature. The clearest diffusion-specific defense ideas I found are disjoint-split dual/student defenses (DualMD/DistillMD), privacy-aware adapter training (MP-LoRA/SMP-LoRA), DIFFENCE-style pre-inference regeneration, and a newer higher-order Langevin sampler defense. The attack side is more crowded and stronger: DRC, black-box variation-API attacks, CLiD, and the 2025 single-query SimA paper all suggest that weak mitigations will be stress-tested hard. ([arXiv][1])

Two negative lessons matter for your selection. First, naive distillation is not automatically private; a 2025 study finds distilled students can have *higher* member-specific leakage than teachers unless distillation is modified. Second, recent diffusion-unlearning work shows that “forgetting” can collapse into concealment unless you audit it aggressively. ([arXiv][2])

My ranking for your setup is:

1. Risk-targeted unlearning.
2. Privacy-aware adapter fine-tuning.
3. Sensitivity-conditioned activation/sampler noise.
4. DistillMD-lite with a bottleneck.
5. Latent bottlenecks as a longer-term architectural line.

## Hypothesis 1: Risk-Targeted SISS Unlearning

* **Defense mechanism:** Use your current GSA/PIA signals to rank the most leakage-prone training members, then run a short post-training unlearning fine-tune only on that top-k set instead of retraining the whole DDPM.
* **Intuition:** Membership leakage is usually concentrated in a small risky tail. If you surgically flatten the score landscape around that tail, you can remove the strongest evidence without paying DP-SGD cost on the whole model.
* **Literature support:** The strongest direct paper here is *Data Unlearning in Diffusion Models*, which introduces Subtracted Importance Sampled Scores (SISS) and frames efficient point-level unlearning for diffusion models with theoretical guarantees. But you should pair it with the “unlearning or concealment” critique, because that paper argues many diffusion unlearning methods only hide the target concept rather than truly erase it. A 2025 cross-domain study also finds that machine unlearning is not inherently an MIA defense; effectiveness depends on the algorithm. ([arXiv][3])
* **Execution plan:** On your admitted 1000-member surface, rank members by current attack risk, select top 16/32/64, and run a bounded unlearning fine-tune from the admitted checkpoint. Evaluate both overall member/non-member separation and a “forgotten subset” slice.
* **Cost:** Best immediate post-training bet. I would budget roughly 1–3 GPU-hours for a bounded pilot on RTX 4070-class hardware; inference stays 1x. That timing is my estimate for your scale, not a reported paper number.
* **Adaptive attacker:** Re-run your current GSA/PIA baselines, then add DRC and SimA. The key is to report both the full 512/512 split and the forgotten subset itself; otherwise concealment can look like success.  ([arXiv][4])
* **Distinctness:** This is post-training editing/unlearning, not DP noise during training and not stochastic masking at inference.

## Hypothesis 2: Privacy-Aware Adapter Fine-Tuning (“SMP-DDPM”)

* **Defense mechanism:** Add tiny LoRA-style or rank-4/rank-8 adapters only to the late U-Net blocks or attention layers of your current DDPM, and optimize denoising loss plus a proxy MIA penalty.
* **Intuition:** GSA/SimA-style attacks are exploiting sharp, sample-specific structure in the denoiser. Instead of drowning all gradients in DP noise, you can explicitly smooth the directions a proxy attacker uses.
* **Literature support:** *Privacy-Preserving Low-Rank Adaptation against Membership Inference Attacks for Latent Diffusion Models* is the best 2024–2025 template for this family; it proposes MP-LoRA/SMP-LoRA and shows that privacy-aware low-rank adaptation can suppress MI leakage while preserving generation quality. SimA is relevant because it argues denoiser norms directly encode training-set proximity, which is exactly the kind of structure you want these adapters to flatten. ([arXiv][5])
* **Execution plan:** Freeze most of the DDPM, attach adapters to the mid block and final up blocks, train a cheap proxy attack on cached ε-norm or gradient features from a 64–128 sample packet, and run 10k–20k adapter steps. If training is unstable, borrow the bottleneck idea from the 2025 KD paper as a stabilizer for the privacy head.
* **Cost:** Strongest non-DP training-time idea that still looks bounded. My estimate is 2–4 GPU-hours for a pilot and about 1.1–1.3x training memory; inference can stay 1x if you merge adapters.
* **Adaptive attacker:** Do defense-aware shadow retraining on the defended checkpoint rather than only threshold retuning. SimA should be in the attack suite because it directly targets the score geometry this defense is trying to smooth. ([arXiv][6])
* **Distinctness:** This is objective shaping via lightweight adapters, not DP-SGD and not inference-time randomization.

## Hypothesis 3: Sensitivity-Conditioned Activation Noise / Sampler Noise

* **Defense mechanism:** Instead of dropout, inject continuous Gaussian noise into selected activations or ε-predictions only when a query/timestep looks high-risk; if that works, escalate to a higher-order Langevin sampler pilot.
* **Intuition:** Dropout destroys coordinates. Additive noise blurs the score field more smoothly and can be targeted only at the timesteps or activations that leak the most, which should directly hurt trajectory-consistency and score-aggregation attacks.
* **Literature support:** *Dynamic Probabilistic Noise Injection for Membership Inference Defense* argues for sensitivity-conditioned inference-time noise rather than fixed noise. The direct diffusion-specific paper in this family is the 2025 higher-order Langevin defense, which introduces auxiliary variables to mix external randomness earlier in the diffusion process. DIFFENCE is also relevant as a no-retrain comparator because it shows that inference-side obfuscation can improve privacy with small overhead. ([arXiv][7])
* **Execution plan:** Use your current PIA traces to identify the most informative timesteps, start with mid-block activation noise or ε-noise on just those steps, and compare fixed vs entropy-conditioned scaling. Treat the Langevin variant as phase 2, because its image-side evidence is thinner.
* **Cost:** Best no-retrain experiment. A pilot is hours, not days. I would expect something between roughly stochastic-dropout-class overhead and moderate extra sampler cost, but there is no DDPM/CIFAR10 timing I can cite yet.
* **Adaptive attacker:** Repeated-query aggregation is the main threat here. Evaluate with 3/5/10 repeats, give the attacker both the mean and variance across runs, and keep the budget fixed so the comparison is honest. Black-box variation-style averaging and SimA are the right challengers. ([arXiv][8])
* **Distinctness:** Continuous noise or altered sampling dynamics, not Bernoulli dropout.

## Hypothesis 4: DistillMD-Lite with a Vulnerability-Aware Bottleneck

* **Defense mechanism:** Split the training data, continue two teacher checkpoints on disjoint halves, and distill a smaller student using only teacher-generated soft targets; add a bottleneck or “non-vulnerable only” filter during distillation.
* **Intuition:** A single sample can only be a member of one teacher. Distillation from disjoint teachers should dilute sample-specific traces in the student more effectively than a single model trained on the whole set.
* **Literature support:** *Dual-Model Defense* is the strongest direct diffusion-defense paper I found for this idea; it proposes DualMD and DistillMD and reports reduced MIA success in both black-box and white-box settings. But the 2025 KD paper is the necessary warning label: naive distillation can worsen member leakage, so the student must be regularized with a bottleneck or vulnerability filter. ([arXiv][1])
* **Execution plan:** For a bounded pilot, do not train three models from scratch. Start from your admitted checkpoint, run short split-specific continuation for two teachers, then distill a smaller student. A 25% data / reduced-epoch smoke test is enough to decide whether this line deserves full budget.
* **Cost:** Highest-upside medium-term defense, but not my first ≤4h sprint. A credible pilot is probably 6–12 GPU-hours on a single 4070-class device; direct DualMD serving also doubles inference.
* **Adaptive attacker:** Attack the student, not just the teachers. The failure mode to watch is exactly what the KD paper found: the student becoming more member-separable than the teacher. ([arXiv][2])
* **Distinctness:** Data splitting plus distillation, not training-time DP noise and not inference-time randomization.

## Hypothesis 5: Stronger Latent Bottleneck / Compression Defense

* **Defense mechanism:** Move from pixel-space DDPM toward a stronger latent bottleneck: a small encoder/decoder wrapper, a latent DDPM, or stronger β-VAE regularization in the latent path.
* **Intuition:** If denoiser norms reveal proximity to nearby training examples, compressing the representation should damp sample-specific spikes before diffusion even starts.
* **Literature support:** The 2025 SimA paper is the key clue here: it reports that Latent Diffusion Models are less vulnerable than pixel-space models because of the latent autoencoder bottleneck, and it explicitly points to stronger β-VAE regularization as a robustness lever. MP-LoRA/SMP-LoRA also shows that privacy-aware adaptation in latent diffusion is viable. ([arXiv][6])
* **Execution plan:** Smallest viable version: train or reuse a compact CIFAR10 autoencoder, then fine-tune a latent diffusion variant and compare it head-to-head with the current pixel DDPM under identical GSA/PIA budgets.
* **Cost:** Best long-term architectural direction, not a fast sprint item. I would treat this as an 8–20 GPU-hour engineering branch before you even reach stable evaluation.
* **Adaptive attacker:** Compare across the same attack suite—current GSA/PIA plus SimA and black-box variation attacks—so that lower leakage is not just an artifact of attack mismatch.  ([arXiv][6])
* **Distinctness:** Architectural compression, not an optimization trick layered onto the same pixel-space DDPM.

## What I would *not* prioritize as standalone bets

I would not make plain early stopping, vanilla augmentation, or naive distillation your headline defenses. CLiD explicitly reports strong resistance to early stopping and data augmentation, and the 2025 KD paper shows that distillation can preserve or even worsen member leakage unless you change how the student is trained. Use augmentation as a stabilizer, not as the main story. ([arXiv][9])

## Adaptive attacker protocol I would standardize

Keep your current bounded adaptive surface from the repo, but strengthen it in three ways: keep the existing repeated-query gray-box protocol, add defense-aware shadow retraining whenever the defense changes model parameters, and always report low-FPR metrics first. External challengers should include DRC, variation-API black-box attacks, CLiD when you move to conditional models, and SimA for single-query score-based pressure tests. Repo memo: . My practical call is simple: run Hypotheses 1–3 first, because they are the only ones that plausibly fit your current DDPM/CIFAR10 surface and a ≤4h bounded pilot. If one of those meaningfully improves **TPR@1%FPR** or **TPR@0.1%FPR** under adaptive evaluation, then spend the bigger budget on DistillMD-lite.
