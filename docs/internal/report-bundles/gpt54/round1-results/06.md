Your notes have the right diagnosis: G1-A is blocked less by the tri-evidence idea itself than by the current requirement for identity-aligned 512/512 surfaces, while TMIA-DM only exists at 256/256. Under your own DDPM/CIFAR10 cost model, recreating TMIA-DM literally with 512 independent shadow models is the expensive path you should avoid if you can.   

What changed in the 2024–2025 literature is that strong MIAs are no longer synonymous with “hundreds of full shadow models.” On the diffusion side, the ICML 2024 quantile-regression attack replaces same-architecture shadow copies with much smaller models and even improves performance by bagging weak attackers; RMIA reports strong low-FPR power with as few as one reference model; BASE matches or beats LiRA/RMIA at lower cost; and SHAPOOL reduces shadow construction cost by training shared models jointly. ([Proceedings of Machine Learning Research][1])

The caution is just as important: recent diffusion papers argue that MIA performance is easy to overestimate under unrealistic evaluation. WACV 2024 and CopyMark warn that diffusion MIAs weaken under fairer setups, and CDI at CVPR 2025 explicitly shifts from single-image claims to dataset-level aggregation, reporting >99% confidence with as few as 70 points. That means the right question is not “How do I fake 512 shadows?” but “What is the cheapest route that preserves low-FPR separation on my own aligned 256-surface, then transfers cleanly to the 512 IDs I already have?” ([OpenReview][2])

I did not find a public paper under the exact name **TMIA-DM**, so I’m treating it as your internal temporal-noise shadow attack as described in the notes. 

## Hypothesis 1: Temporal quantile-regression surrogate for TMIA-DM

* **Method**: Replace exact TMIA-DM with a target-only temporal surrogate. For each sample, compute a compact temporal feature vector from the target DDPM at fixed timesteps—examples: reconstruction loss, residual norm, early/late loss ratio, slope, curvature, and variance across timesteps—then fit quantile-regression heads to predict the nonmember tail. Calibrate the surrogate to your existing 256-sample TMIA-DM scores with isotonic regression or quantile matching before plugging it into G1-A. ([Proceedings of Machine Learning Research][1])  

* **Intuition**: This keeps the part G1-A actually needs—good rank ordering and tail separation at low FPR—without paying for 512 same-architecture shadows. The diffusion QR paper is the strongest direct support: it learns example-specific thresholds, uses only much smaller models, and improves with a bag of weak attackers. CLiD supports the broader idea that a lower-stochasticity analytical signal can outperform heavier stochastic membership estimators, and the 2025 feature-density paper shows low-FPR gains on diffusion models without many shadows. ([Proceedings of Machine Learning Research][1])

* **Execution plan**: Train and validate only on your existing aligned 256-surface. First, extract temporal features for the 256 member/nonmember IDs. Second, fit 8–16 small QR models on bootstrapped folds and aggregate them. Third, calibrate the aggregated score to TMIA-DM-256. Fourth, score the already-existing 512 IDs directly from the target model, which automatically satisfies identity alignment because you are evaluating exactly the same sample IDs as PIA/CDI.  

* **Cost**: Roughly one batched pass over the target DDPM across a small timestep grid plus tiny regression models. Relative to your 2048–4096 GPU-hour full-shadow baseline, this is plausibly well under 1% of the compute and is the only route here that realistically fits your “CPU-first / ≤4h GPU on a 4070” constraint.  

* **Validity**: Use the quality gates you already sketched: Spearman/Pearson rank preservation, AUC difference, TPR@1%FPR difference, threshold stability, and—most importantly—whether the tri-score still beats the components at low FPR after substitution. I would make `ρ > 0.8`, `|ΔAUC| < 0.05`, and `|ΔTPR@1%FPR| < 0.05` the promotion bar.  

## Hypothesis 2: RMIA/BASE-style temporal likelihood-ratio calibration with very few references

* **Method**: Define a scalar temporal statistic (S(x)) from the target model—say late-window mean residual, temporal slope, or a learned combination of temporal features—and turn it into a likelihood-ratio attack. Estimate the null/reference distribution with either 1–8 lightweight reference models or an analytical null, then compute a temporal LR score instead of a shadow-counted TMIA-DM score. ([OpenReview][3])

* **Intuition**: Recent likelihood-ratio MIAs show that low-FPR power does not inherently require hundreds of references. RMIA explicitly reports good performance with as few as one pre-trained reference model, BASE matches or exceeds LiRA/RMIA at lower cost, and Gaussian Membership Inference Privacy derives an analytical attack that requires no shadow models at all. That makes this the most principled “few-reference” replacement if you want to keep LR-style semantics rather than moving to a purely learned surrogate. ([OpenReview][3])

* **Execution plan**: Use the 256-sample aligned surface to compare several temporal statistics, pick the one with the best low-FPR stability, estimate a reference/null model, then freeze it and score the 512 IDs. Because you only need the target score plus a few reference statistics, identity alignment is trivial once the statistic is defined on the 512 sample IDs. 

* **Cost**: If you use an analytical null or 1–2 small references, this is still bounded and probably within a few percent of the 512-shadow baseline. If you insist on full DDPM references, it quickly stops being laptop-friendly, so I would only pursue this after Hypothesis 1. 

* **Validity**: Same gates as H1, plus sensitivity analysis over the number of references. If the score moves materially when going from 1 to 2 to 4 references, reject it as under-calibrated. 

## Hypothesis 3: Query-efficient perturbation/feature-density temporal signal as the third evidence stream

* **Method**: Stop trying to reproduce TMIA-DM exactly and instead define a cheap third signal from perturbation robustness. Apply mild masking, patch-drop, or feature removal to each image, then measure how temporal losses or trajectory statistics change under perturbation. Use that robustness curve as the third evidence source alongside PIA and CDI. ([OpenReview][4])

* **Intuition**: The 2025 feature-density paper is directly relevant because it targets low-FPR performance, does not need many shadows, and reports gains on diffusion models. PIA already tells you that temporal trajectories contain membership signal; this hypothesis asks whether a perturbation-based temporal signal is complementary enough to serve as the missing third stream. CLiD is another sign that cheaper analytical indicators can remain useful even when augmentation or regularization weakens heavier attacks. ([OpenReview][4])

* **Execution plan**: On the aligned 256 surface, compute K perturbed views per sample, derive a compact robustness score, and test whether it adds information beyond PIA/CDI. Only promote it if it improves the tri-score over PIA+CDI; otherwise it is just a more expensive duplicate of PIA. 

* **Cost**: A few dozen target-model queries per sample, no shadow training. Still plausibly within your 4h-GPU ceiling if K stays small and batched. The main risk here is not compute but redundancy with PIA. ([OpenReview][4])

* **Validity**: In addition to the usual AUC/TPR checks, require incremental value over PIA alone. If the third stream is highly collinear with PIA and does not move low-FPR metrics, drop it. 

## Hypothesis 4: Small shared/adapted shadow pool plus bootstrap, not 512 independent trainings

* **Method**: If you must remain in the shadow-model family, do not train 512 independent DDPMs. Train a small shared pool—either SHAPOOL-style shared shadows, or one base DDPM with lightweight branch/adapters—and then bootstrap the resulting score population into 128–512 virtual calibrants. Use sequential stopping so you stop when thresholds stabilize, not when an arbitrary shadow count is reached. ([OpenReview][5])

* **Intuition**: This is the most faithful route to “TMIA-DM-like” semantics while still reducing cost. SHAPOOL directly targets shadow-model inefficiency; ProLoRA shows diffusion adapters can transfer without retraining; DEFT shows diffusion fine-tuning can be made small-network-only; and safe testing gives a clean statistical framework for optional continuation without losing type-I control. ([OpenReview][5])

* **Execution plan**: Train 8–32 shared/adapted shadows on stratified subsets, score the 512 IDs, bootstrap the score population, and keep increasing the pool only until threshold CV and low-FPR metrics stabilize on the 256 teacher surface. This is the literature-backed version of the bootstrap idea in your roadmap. 

* **Cost**: Far cheaper than 512 independent shadows, but still not CPU-first. On a single 4070 this is a multi-day fallback, not the first thing I would try. Think “tens to low hundreds of GPU-hours,” not “a few hours.”  

* **Validity**: You need more than attack metrics. Also check diversity of branches/shared shadows, threshold CV, and whether bootstrap tails match the teacher surface well enough to preserve TPR@1%FPR. 

## Hypothesis 5: Do not use TMIA-DM at all; pivot to CDI-style set-level evidence

* **Method**: Change the milestone rather than forcing a per-sample TMIA-DM substitute. Aggregate PIA + CDI + a cheap temporal surrogate over a set of candidate points and make a set-level claim, not an individual-point claim. This moves G1-A from “per-sample tri-evidence” to “set-level bounded auxiliary evidence.” ([CVPR 2026][6]) 

* **Intuition**: CDI’s core message is that diffusion MIAs are often too weak pointwise, but useful when aggregated across a dataset. That is consistent with CopyMark and the WACV realism paper, both of which caution against overclaiming from individual-image membership tests on diffusion models. If your real goal is “defensible evidence of data use,” this is arguably stronger than fighting to preserve exact TMIA-DM semantics. ([CVPR 2026][6])

* **Execution plan**: Define blocks of candidate points, aggregate standardized scores, and use a formal dataset-level test. CDI reports strong confidence with as few as 70 points, so this can be practical even when individual-point evidence is weak. ([CVPR 2026][6])

* **Cost**: Near-zero new training. This is the cheapest path by far. 

* **Validity**: The downside is semantic, not statistical: this does not unblock X-90 exactly as currently defined, unless you explicitly redefine promotion around set-level evidence. 

## My actual recommendation

I would rank them **H1 > H2 > H5 > H4 > H3** for your current constraints. H1 is the best fit to your hardware and to the actual blocker because it avoids identity mismatch entirely, can be validated on the 256 teacher surface you already have, and is directly supported by the strongest 2024 diffusion paper in this area. H2 is the most principled fallback if you want to stay closer to likelihood-ratio MIAs. H5 is the strongest “don’t force TMIA-DM” option. H4 is the best shadow-faithful fallback, but not a laptop-class one. H3 is interesting, but I would only keep it if it proves complementary to PIA rather than re-measuring the same thing. ([Proceedings of Machine Learning Research][1])

The part of your internal roadmap I would change is the default recommendation. Your 64-base bootstrap plan is directionally sensible as a shadow-faithful fallback, but the public evidence is stronger for **changing the calibration family** than for **emulating exact TMIA-DM with virtual shadows**. I would therefore make “temporal QR surrogate calibrated on TMIA-DM-256” the default, and demote “64 real shadows + bootstrap” to Plan B.  ([Proceedings of Machine Learning Research][1])

A compact theoretical read is: 512 is probably **not** a universal statistical necessity. Under the simple threshold-estimation bound (N \ge \ln(2/\delta)/(2\varepsilon^2)), 512 corresponds to about a 6% threshold error at 95% confidence, while 128 corresponds to about 12%. Under a simple 80%-power two-sample approximation, 512 total samples corresponds to a small standardized effect around (d \approx 0.25). So 512 looks more like a conservative engineering choice for a weak-signal tail-calibration problem than a theorem of membership inference. Your own PAC/power notes point in the same direction.  

What I could not substantiate from public 2024–2025 work is a diffusion paper that proves **64 real shadows + bootstrap = 512 independent DDPM shadows** specifically for low-FPR tail calibration, or a direct public paper on **TMIA-DM** by that name. The strongest public evidence I found supports replacing or relaxing the shadow-heavy calibration step, not faithfully recreating it.
